Chemical Reaction Dynamics (Langevin Model)

Understanding the Units

In computational chemistry, simulations are often run using Reduced (Dimensionless) Units. This prevents computers from making rounding errors when dealing with extremely small numbers.

  • Energy (Barrier & Asymmetry): Expressed relative to a baseline thermal energy. A barrier of 5.0 means it takes 5 times the baseline thermal energy to cross the transition state.
  • Temperature (T): If T=1.0, the average thermal energy equals 1 energy unit. If T=5.0, the system is 5 times hotter.
  • Mass (m): Fixed at 1.0 reduced unit to cleanly demonstrate kinetic behavior across this potential energy surface.
  • Distance (x): The reaction coordinate is represented in reduced length units (similar to Angstroms).

Langevin Dynamics

The movement of each particle is governed by Langevin dynamics, which modifies classical physics to account for an environment filled with a surrounding solvent (like water or honey). The motion you see is a tug-of-war between four factors:

  • The Landscape (Deterministic Force): Particles naturally want to roll "downhill" toward the lowest energy state (the products or reactants).
  • The Friction (Viscous Damping): As the particle moves, the solvent pushes back, causing drag. High damping means the particle moves sluggishly; low damping allows it to coast freely.
  • The Thermal Kicks (Stochastic Noise): Invisible solvent molecules are constantly bombarding the particle from all directions. This provides the random kicks of energy needed to occasionally push the particle up and over the energy barrier.
  • The Inertia (Mass): The fundamental tendency of the particle to maintain its current motion, carrying it forward through the solvent.
